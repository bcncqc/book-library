The Five Dysfunctions of a Team

Patrick Lencioni
Team dysfunction cascades in a predictable order — absence of trust breeds fear of conflict, which breeds lack of commitment, avoidance of accountability, and inattention to results, each one caused by the layer above it.

1Core Thesis

Told as a business fable about a struggling executive team, Lencioni's model presents five team dysfunctions as a cascading pyramid, where each dysfunction causes the one above it: absence of trust (people hide weaknesses and mistakes) leads to fear of conflict (artificial harmony instead of real debate), which leads to lack of commitment (ambiguity about decisions), which leads to avoidance of accountability (tolerating poor performance), which leads to inattention to results (individual ego or status prioritized over the team's collective goals). Fixing a team requires working through the pyramid from the base up, not addressing the visible top-level symptom directly.

2Key Ideas

  • Absence of Trust. Team members who don't feel safe being vulnerable (admitting mistakes, asking for help) with each other can't build the foundation everything else depends on.
  • Fear of Conflict. Without trust, teams default to artificial harmony — avoiding real debate — rather than passionate, productive disagreement about ideas.
  • Lack of Commitment. Without real debate surfacing genuine buy-in, teams achieve only ambiguous, halfhearted commitment to decisions, which erodes execution.
  • Avoidance of Accountability. Without genuine commitment to a decision, team members hesitate to hold each other accountable for behaviors and performance that undermine it.
  • Inattention to Results. Without accountability, individual ego, status, or department-level concerns take priority over the team's actual collective results.
  • Fix from the base, not the top. An organization that jumps straight to "hold people accountable" without first building trust and real conflict is treating a symptom, not the cause.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Five Dysfunctions Pyramid

Absence of Trust → Fear of Conflict → Lack of Commitment → Avoidance of Accountability → Inattention to Results — each dysfunction is caused by the layer beneath it, meaning a fix has to start at the base, not the visible symptom at the top.

Leadership translation: A team that avoids holding each other accountable often isn't lacking a policy — it's usually lacking the trust and real debate that would have produced genuine commitment to the standard in the first place.

Artificial Harmony vs. Productive Conflict

Teams that avoid visible disagreement to preserve surface-level harmony are usually masking a lack of trust, and end up with weaker decisions than teams that debate genuinely and directly.

Example: A team meeting where everyone nods along without real pushback often produces a weaker decision than one with visible, respectful disagreement.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Diagnose which layer of the pyramid your team is actually stuck at before addressing the visible symptom
  • Build trust deliberately before expecting genuine conflict or commitment
  • Encourage real, respectful debate rather than settling for artificial harmony

How to do it

  • Before adding an accountability policy, check whether the team actually has the trust and genuine commitment underneath it
  • Model your own vulnerability (admitting a mistake or gap) to invite the same from the team
  • Explicitly invite disagreement in team meetings rather than treating silence as agreement

When to apply it

  • Diagnosing a newly formed or newly restructured team's dynamics
  • Addressing an accountability gap that isn't responding to policy alone
  • Building genuine buy-in for a major decision, not just surface-level agreement

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Team dysfunctions cascade — each one is caused by the layer beneath it.
  • Without trust, teams avoid real conflict and settle for artificial harmony.
  • Without real debate, commitment to decisions stays ambiguous and weak.
  • An accountability problem is usually really a trust and commitment problem underneath.

6Real-World Applications

Diagnosing a Newly Restructured Team

Assessing which layer of the pyramid a newly formed team is actually stuck at — often trust, given a recent departure or reorganization — clarifies where to actually invest before expecting accountability or results.

Building Genuine Buy-In for a Decision

Inviting real, visible disagreement in a decision meeting, rather than accepting silence as agreement, tends to produce more durable commitment than a decision reached through artificial harmony.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Addressing the visible top-level symptom (poor results, weak accountability) without fixing the trust and conflict layers beneath it.
  • Treating silence in a meeting as genuine agreement.
  • Adding a policy or process to force accountability without the underlying trust and commitment to support it.
