1Core Thesis

The organizing idea common to "science of scaling"-type literature is that growth isn't a single, continuous curve you push harder on — it's a series of distinct stages, each with its own binding constraint (the resource, process, or capability most limiting further growth at that size). What worked to get from stage A to B (founder hustle, informal coordination, a single great salesperson) often actively breaks the business at stage B to C. Scaling well means diagnosing the current binding constraint accurately and building the specific capability that resolves it, rather than applying more effort to the previous stage's playbook.

2Key Ideas

  • Growth happens in stages, not a smooth curve. Each stage (startup, early growth, scale-up, maturity) has a different binding constraint, and the skills that worked for the last stage often don't transfer.
  • Diagnose the actual constraint before investing. Common candidates: lead generation, sales capacity, delivery/fulfillment capacity, management bandwidth, or process/data maturity — throwing resources at the wrong one wastes the investment.
  • What got you here won't get you there. Informal, founder-driven coordination that works at a small scale becomes the bottleneck at a larger one, and has to be deliberately replaced.
  • Systemization compounds. Turning a working ad hoc process into a documented, repeatable one is what allows it to scale past the original person who invented it.
  • Data visibility is a scaling prerequisite. Growing organizations need shared, reliable metrics to coordinate without every decision requiring direct founder/leader oversight.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Binding Constraint

At any given size, one specific limiting factor caps growth more than any other — identify and resolve that one first, since fixing anything else won't move the ceiling.

Sales-leadership translation: Before investing further in lead generation or headcount, ask whether NA's actual binding constraint right now is process/role clarity rather than volume — fixing the wrong constraint wastes the investment.

Stage-Based Growth

Organizations pass through distinct stages, each requiring different capabilities — informal coordination that works with 5 people usually fails at 50, and needs to be deliberately replaced rather than stretched further.

Example: Many companies' transition from startup to growth stage is exactly this shift — informal "everyone does everything" coordination needs to become defined roles and documented process.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Diagnose the current binding constraint honestly before allocating resources
  • Systemize what currently works before trying to scale it further
  • Build shared metrics so coordination doesn't require constant leader oversight

How to do it

  • Ask "if we fixed everything except X, would growth actually increase?" for each candidate constraint
  • Document a process before adding headcount to run more of it
  • Define 2-3 shared metrics per function that reduce the need for direct oversight

When to apply it

  • Annual/quarterly planning when deciding where to invest next
  • Before scaling headcount or territory in a given function
  • Any time a process that worked informally starts breaking under volume

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Not every growth problem is a "do more" problem — find the actual constraint first.
  • The playbook that got you here often breaks at the next size — expect to replace it, not just scale it.
  • Systemizing a process is what lets it survive past its original owner.
  • Shared metrics reduce how much a growing org needs its leader in every decision.

6Real-World Applications

Diagnosing NA's Real Constraint

Before investing further in headcount or new accounts, this framework argues for confirming that role clarity/process — not volume — is the actual binding constraint on the team's growth right now.

Startup-to-Growth Transition

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Investing in headcount or volume before confirming that's the actual constraint.
  • Assuming the previous stage's playbook will keep working at the next size.
  • Scaling an undocumented process instead of systemizing it first.
