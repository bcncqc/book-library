Blue Ocean Strategy

W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne
Instead of fighting competitors for share of an existing, bloody market ("red ocean"), the highest-value move is creating uncontested new market space ("blue ocean") where competition is irrelevant.

1Core Thesis

Kim and Mauborgne argue that sustainable, high-growth strategy usually doesn't come from out-competing rivals within an existing industry's boundaries (a "red ocean," bloodied by competition) — it comes from redefining the market itself to create uncontested space (a "blue ocean") where the old rules of competition don't apply. The path there is value innovation: simultaneously pursuing differentiation and low cost by reconstructing what factors an industry competes on, rather than accepting the existing value/cost trade-off as fixed.

2Key Ideas

  • Red oceans vs. blue oceans. Red oceans are existing markets where companies fight over the same customers; blue oceans are new demand created by redefining the game.
  • Value innovation, not value trade-off. Conventional strategy assumes you must choose between differentiation and low cost; blue ocean strategy pursues both by changing which factors matter.
  • The Four Actions Framework: Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create. A tool for systematically rethinking an industry's value curve — what factors can be eliminated entirely, reduced below industry standard, raised above it, and created that the industry has never offered.
  • Non-customers matter as much as customers. Blue oceans are often found by understanding why people who don't currently buy in your industry stay away, not just by studying existing customers more closely.
  • Competing on the existing industry's terms caps your upside. As long as you're optimizing within the accepted rules, you're fighting for share of a market whose total size is fixed.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Four Actions Framework

Eliminate factors the industry takes for granted but that add little value; Reduce factors below industry standard; Raise factors above industry standard; Create factors the industry has never offered — used together to build a new value curve.

Leadership translation: Instead of matching a competitor feature-for-feature, ask what could be eliminated entirely from the standard offering and what genuinely new value could be created instead.

The Strategy Canvas

A visual plot of an industry's competing factors and how each player performs on them — used to spot where every competitor is clustered on the same curve, revealing the opportunity for a genuinely different one.

Example: If every player in a category competes on the same three or four factors, that clustering itself is the signal that a differently-shaped offering could open new space.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Map your industry's current competing factors on a strategy canvas
  • Apply Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create systematically rather than incrementally matching competitors
  • Study why non-customers stay away, not just what current customers want more of

How to do it

  • List the 4-6 factors your category competes on and plot where every major player sits on each
  • For each factor, ask: eliminate, reduce, raise, or create?
  • Interview or research a segment that considered but rejected your category entirely

When to apply it

  • Positioning a differentiated offering against entrenched competitors
  • Annual strategy work, especially in a crowded or commoditizing category
  • Evaluating whether to compete harder within existing rules or redefine them

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Fighting harder in a crowded market caps your upside.
  • You don't have to choose between differentiation and low cost if you change which factors matter.
  • Non-customers can reveal a bigger opportunity than your existing customers.
  • Matching competitors feature-for-feature keeps you in the red ocean.

6Real-World Applications

Positioning Against Entrenched Competitors

Rather than matching an established competitor's feature set point for point, mapping the category's shared competing factors and applying Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create can surface a genuinely differentiated position.

Expanding Beyond Current Customers

Understanding why a segment considered but didn't adopt your category at all often points toward a bigger opportunity than incremental improvements aimed at existing customers.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming you must trade off differentiation against cost.
  • Only studying existing customers instead of understanding non-customers too.
  • Competing by matching rivals feature-for-feature instead of reshaping the value curve.
