Behavioral Design · Habit Formation

Hooked

Nir Eyal
Habit-forming products aren't built through advertising alone — they're engineered through a repeating loop of trigger, action, variable reward, and investment that gets easier to re-enter each time.

1Core Thesis

Eyal argues that the most habit-forming products succeed not through advertising spend but through a designed behavioral loop — the "Hook Model" — that gets a user in with minimal friction and gradually makes returning easier and more automatic each cycle. Unlike a single persuasive pitch, the Hook Model works through repetition: an external or eventually internal trigger prompts an action, which is followed by a variable (unpredictable) reward, and the user makes some small investment that increases the odds they'll return and re-enter the loop again.

2Key Ideas

  • The Hook Model: Trigger, Action, Variable Reward, Investment. A repeating four-step loop that builds habitual engagement over time, rather than relying on one-off persuasion.
  • External triggers become internal triggers. Early on, a notification or reminder (external trigger) prompts the behavior; over enough repetitions, an internal cue (boredom, uncertainty, a specific emotion) alone becomes enough to prompt it.
  • Variable rewards are more compelling than predictable ones. Uncertainty about exactly what reward is coming next (a like, a message, new information) keeps engagement higher than a completely predictable reward would.
  • Small investments increase the odds of returning. Each bit of effort, data, or content a user puts into a product (not just what they get out of it) makes the product more valuable to them specifically and more likely they'll come back.
  • Manufactured habits carry an ethical responsibility. Eyal explicitly frames the model as something to be used responsibly — habit-forming design that doesn't genuinely serve the user's interest raises real ethical concerns.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Hook Model

Trigger (external or internal cue) → Action (the simplest behavior done in anticipation of reward) → Variable Reward (an unpredictable payoff that satisfies the need) → Investment (the user puts something in, increasing the likelihood of returning) — then the cycle repeats, often with the investment step loading the next trigger.

Leadership translation: A team adopting a new CRM or process habit needs its own version of this loop — a reliable trigger (a specific moment in the workflow), an easy first action, some kind of payoff, and a small investment (updated data, a completed field) that makes returning to it easier next time.

Variable Reward

Rewards that are somewhat unpredictable in timing or content sustain engagement more effectively than fully predictable ones — the brain stays more engaged anticipating an uncertain payoff.

Example: A recognition program with some genuine variability in timing or form tends to sustain more engagement than a purely mechanical, fully predictable one.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Design a clear, low-friction trigger for any new habit or process you want adopted
  • Build in some form of reward for engaging with the new behavior, ideally with some variability
  • Design a small "investment" step that makes returning to the behavior easier next time

How to do it

  • Attach a new habit to an existing, reliable moment in someone's workflow (an external trigger) rather than expecting spontaneous adoption
  • Make the first action required as small and frictionless as possible
  • Design a step where the user contributes something (data, a note, a preference) that increases the tool's value to them specifically next time

When to apply it

  • Driving adoption of a new internal tool, CRM habit, or process
  • Designing customer-facing product engagement loops
  • Diagnosing why a well-designed process still isn't sticking

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Habits form through a repeating loop, not a single persuasive push.
  • Uncertainty about the reward keeps people more engaged than predictability does.
  • Small user investments make the next return more likely.
  • A clear, easy trigger matters more than most people assume when building a new habit.

6Real-World Applications

Driving CRM or Process Adoption

Attaching a new required habit (like updating a CRM field) to an existing, reliable moment in a rep's workflow — rather than a standalone ask — applies the Hook Model's trigger design directly.

Designing Recognition or Incentive Programs

Building in some genuine variability to how and when recognition happens tends to sustain more engagement than a fully predictable, mechanical program.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Expecting a new habit to form without a clear, reliable trigger.
  • Making a fully predictable reward system, which sustains less engagement than some variability would.
  • Skipping the "investment" step, leaving nothing that makes returning to the behavior easier.
