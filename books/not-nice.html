Confidence · Authenticity

Not Nice

Dr. Aziz Gazipura
"Niceness" is often a fear-driven strategy to avoid disapproval, not genuine kindness — real confidence means being authentic even when it risks someone's disapproval.

1Core Thesis

Gazipura draws a hard line between "Nice" (a compulsive, fear-based pattern of people-pleasing, conflict avoidance, and self-erasure meant to prevent disapproval) and genuine kindness (a values-driven choice to treat people well, which can coexist with directness, boundaries, and saying no). Chronic Niceness is rooted in a deep, often unexamined fear of being disliked, rejected, or seen as selfish — and it quietly costs people their authentic voice, their boundaries, and often their credibility, since others can sense when agreement is fear-based rather than genuine.

2Key Ideas

  • Nice vs. Kind. Niceness is compulsive and fear-driven — you can't not do it without anxiety. Kindness is a free choice grounded in values, and it doesn't preclude directness or disagreement.
  • Approval-seeking is the hidden engine behind most "Nice" behavior. The core fear is usually some version of "if I'm direct/say no/disagree, I'll be rejected or seen as a bad person."
  • Confidence is built by acting despite fear, not by eliminating fear first. Waiting to feel confident before speaking up gets it backward — the action itself is what builds the confidence.
  • Boundary-setting is a skill, not a personality trait. Saying no, disagreeing, and asking for what you want are learnable behaviors, not something you either have or don't.
  • People respect directness more than compulsive agreement, even when they initially push back on it — chronic agreement often reads as unreliable or hollow over time.
  • Approval addiction has a cost that compounds. Every time Niceness overrides an honest opinion or boundary, it reinforces the belief that your authentic self isn't acceptable — a self-perpetuating loop.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Nice vs. Kind Distinction

A 2x2 lens: Nice is compulsive and fear-based; Kind is chosen and values-based. The test is whether you could say no or disagree in the moment without significant anxiety — if not, it's Nice, not Kind.

Sales-leadership translation: Agreeing to an unreasonable channel-partner ask because saying no feels uncomfortable is Niceness. Saying no clearly, while still treating the partner with respect, is Kindness — and it's usually the more credible position long-term.

Act-First Confidence Building

Confidence isn't a precondition for bold action — it's a byproduct of having taken bold action repeatedly despite fear. Waiting to feel ready before being direct keeps the avoidance pattern intact indefinitely.

Example: Giving a direct report hard feedback for the first time will feel uncomfortable regardless of preparation — the discomfort doesn't go away until after you've done it several times, not before.

The Approval-Seeking Cost Ledger

Every instance of Niceness overriding your real opinion has a cost — lost credibility, a slightly more eroded sense of your own authentic voice, and a slightly stronger habit loop. Naming the cost explicitly makes the tradeoff visible instead of "free."

Example: Not addressing a direct report's recurring lateness because you don't want the awkward conversation costs you both the team's respect for the standard and the report's chance to actually improve.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Notice when agreement or silence is fear-based rather than a genuine choice
  • Practice small, low-stakes instances of directness to build the muscle
  • Treat discomfort as expected, not as a sign to back off

How to do it

  • Before agreeing to something, pause and ask "would I still say yes if disapproval wasn't a risk?"
  • Deliver one direct piece of feedback or one clear "no" this week, specifically, as practice
  • Expect the anxiety to show up and act anyway — it's the mechanism, not a signal to stop

When to apply it

  • Any negotiation with a channel partner where the instinct is to concede to avoid friction
  • Delivering hard feedback to a direct report
  • Setting boundaries with leadership about scope, timeline, or resourcing

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Being "nice" out of fear isn't the same as being kind — one is compulsive, the other is a choice.
  • Confidence comes after action, not before it — you build it by doing the uncomfortable thing anyway.
  • Chronic agreement erodes your credibility over time, even though it feels safe in the moment.
  • Saying no is a skill you build with repetition, not a fixed trait some people have and others don't.

6Real-World Applications

Channel Negotiations

Conceding to a channel partner's asks to avoid friction is Niceness dressed as diplomacy — real kindness (and better outcomes) come from being direct about what the org can and can't do.

Hard Conversations with Direct Reports

Delaying a difficult performance conversation because it feels uncomfortable is the exact pattern this book targets — the discomfort is the mechanism, not a signal to wait longer.

Managing Up

Pushing back on an unrealistic ask from senior leadership, respectfully but directly, is Kindness to the org's actual constraints — agreeing to avoid friction just moves the problem downstream.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Mistaking compulsive agreement for good relationship management — it usually erodes trust over time instead of building it.
  • Waiting to feel confident before being direct — the confidence comes after, not before.
  • Avoiding a hard conversation indefinitely because the discomfort never fully goes away on its own.
  • Treating boundary-setting as something you either have or don't, rather than a skill you build with practice.
