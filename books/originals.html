Originals

Adam Grant
People who champion new ideas successfully aren't reckless risk-takers — they're often more cautious than assumed, hedging their bets while pushing a genuinely nonconforming idea forward.

1Core Thesis

Grant argues against the myth that original thinkers are natural-born risk-takers with unshakable confidence in their ideas. His research finds the opposite: many of history's most successful originals felt real doubt, procrastinated strategically, and hedged their personal risk (keeping a stable job while testing a new idea) while still pushing forward on genuinely nonconforming work. Championing original ideas inside an organization is a learnable skill involving specific tactics — timing, framing, coalition-building — not just personal boldness.

2Key Ideas

  • Originals often hedge their risk, they don't eliminate it. Many successful innovators kept a stable source of income or backup plan while pursuing their original idea, rather than going all-in immediately.
  • Strategic procrastination can improve original work. Deliberately delaying (within reason) allows more incubation time for divergent thinking, sometimes producing more creative results than rushing to finish.
  • Being first isn't necessary — being better often matters more. Many successful "original" products or ideas were actually not first to market; they improved on and outlasted earlier, less-refined attempts.
  • Selling an original idea requires strategic framing. How an idea is pitched (to whom, in what order, with what framing of risk) significantly affects whether it gets adopted, regardless of its underlying merit.
  • Powerful allies and coalition-building matter for adoption. A genuinely good original idea still often needs organizational sponsorship and the right internal allies to actually gain traction.
  • Doubt about your idea, paired with confidence in your ability to improve it, produces the best original work. Certainty is often a sign you've stopped iterating, not a sign you've gotten it right.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Hedge, Don't Eliminate Risk

Successful originals often maintain a stable fallback while pursuing a nonconforming idea, rather than betting everything at once — reducing personal risk without reducing commitment to the idea itself.

Leadership translation: Piloting a genuinely nonconforming new process on a small scale, while keeping the existing process running, mirrors this hedge — you can commit fully to testing the idea without betting the whole team's output on it immediately.

Strategic Framing for Adoption

Getting an original idea adopted inside an organization depends significantly on how, to whom, and in what order it's pitched — not just on the idea's underlying quality.

Example: Pitching a new process to a skeptical stakeholder first, addressing their specific concern directly, can build momentum that a broad announcement wouldn't.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Pilot a genuinely new idea at small scale while keeping a stable fallback running
  • Build a coalition of allies before pushing a nonconforming idea broadly
  • Treat lingering doubt about your own idea as a sign to keep iterating, not a reason to abandon it

How to do it

  • Test a new process or idea on a small pilot group before rolling it out organization-wide
  • Identify one or two internal allies who'd be persuaded by the idea and involve them before a broader pitch
  • Give yourself permission to keep refining an idea rather than treating early doubt as disqualifying

When to apply it

  • Pitching a genuinely new process, tool, or strategic direction internally
  • Deciding how much personal or team risk to take on a new idea
  • Building buy-in for a change that departs from "how things are usually done"

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Successful originals often hedge their risk rather than going all-in.
  • Being first matters less than being better and more refined.
  • How you pitch an idea affects adoption as much as the idea's actual quality.
  • Doubt about your own idea can be a sign you're still improving it, not a reason to quit.

6Real-World Applications

Piloting a New Process

Testing a genuinely new SOP or workflow with a small pilot group before rolling it out broadly hedges the risk while still committing to testing the idea seriously.

Building Buy-In for Change

Identifying the right internal allies and sequencing who hears about a new idea first can significantly improve adoption odds compared to a single broad announcement.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Going all-in on a new idea without any hedge or small-scale test first.
  • Assuming a good idea will sell itself without strategic framing or allies.
  • Abandoning an idea at the first sign of your own doubt, instead of using it to keep refining.
