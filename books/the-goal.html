Operations · Constraint Theory

The Goal

Eliyahu M. Goldratt
Every system has exactly one bottleneck constraining its total output at any given time — improving anything else feels productive but doesn't actually increase throughput.

1Core Thesis

Told as a business novel about a plant manager racing to save his factory, Goldratt's Theory of Constraints argues that any process — manufacturing or otherwise — has exactly one binding constraint limiting its total output at a given time. Optimizing any step other than that constraint feels like progress but doesn't move overall throughput; the entire system should be managed around identifying, exploiting, and then elevating that one bottleneck, in a repeating cycle, rather than pursuing generic "efficiency" everywhere at once.

2Key Ideas

  • Every system has one binding constraint at a time. Only one step actually limits total throughput — everything else has slack, whether or not it looks busy.
  • Local efficiency isn't the same as system throughput. A non-bottleneck step running at full efficiency can still produce zero improvement in the overall output if the bottleneck hasn't moved.
  • The Five Focusing Steps. Identify the constraint, exploit it (get maximum output from it without new investment), subordinate everything else to that decision, elevate the constraint (invest to increase its capacity), then repeat — because once resolved, a new constraint emerges elsewhere.
  • Throughput, Inventory, and Operating Expense. Goldratt reframes the goal of a business around throughput (rate of generating money through sales) rather than cost-cutting or local efficiency metrics alone.
  • An hour lost at the bottleneck is an hour lost for the whole system. Time lost anywhere else can often be recovered; time lost at the actual constraint cannot.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Five Focusing Steps

Identify the constraint → Exploit it fully before investing further → Subordinate every other decision to that exploitation → Elevate the constraint (invest to increase capacity) → Repeat, since a new constraint will emerge once the old one is resolved.

Leadership translation: If the real constraint on a commercial team's growth is management bandwidth, not lead volume, adding more leads won't increase throughput — the constraint has to be addressed directly first.

Local Efficiency vs. System Throughput

Making a non-bottleneck resource busier or more "efficient" can actually hurt overall performance by creating excess inventory or work-in-progress that doesn't reach the market any faster.

Example: Pushing every rep to generate more top-of-funnel leads doesn't increase revenue if the actual constraint is deal-desk approval turnaround, not lead volume.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Identify the actual constraint on your team or process before optimizing anything else
  • Get maximum output from the constraint before investing in more capacity elsewhere
  • Subordinate other decisions to protecting the constraint's throughput

How to do it

  • Map your process end to end and find the one step where work piles up waiting
  • Before adding headcount or tools anywhere else, confirm that step is actually the constraint
  • Protect the constrained step from unrelated interruptions and low-value work

When to apply it

  • Diagnosing why growth has stalled despite more effort elsewhere
  • Deciding where to invest next (headcount, tools, process) in a growing team
  • Any recurring bottleneck that keeps reappearing despite unrelated fixes

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Only one step actually limits your total output at any given time.
  • Making a non-bottleneck step busier doesn't increase overall throughput.
  • Fix the real constraint first — everything else is a distraction until you do.
  • Once you fix one constraint, a new one appears — the cycle repeats.

6Real-World Applications

Diagnosing a Growth Stall

Before adding more headcount or lead volume, mapping the actual process end to end to find where work backs up identifies the real constraint — often a very different place than the obvious one.

Prioritizing Where to Invest

Investment decisions (a new hire, a new tool, added process) are best evaluated against whether they actually address the current binding constraint, not just whichever function is asking loudest.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Optimizing a step that isn't actually the constraint.
  • Confusing local busyness with real throughput improvement.
  • Investing in capacity everywhere instead of exploiting the actual bottleneck first.
