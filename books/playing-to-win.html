Strategy · Choice Cascade

Playing to Win

A.G. Lafley & Roger Martin
Strategy is a small, interlocking cascade of five choices — and skipping any one of them (especially "how will we win") turns strategy into a wish list instead of a real plan.

1Core Thesis

Lafley (former P&G CEO) and Martin frame strategy as an integrated cascade of five interconnected choices, each of which constrains and clarifies the next: what's your winning aspiration, where will you play, how will you win there, what capabilities do you need, and what management systems will support them. Their central argument is that strategy fails most often not from a bad individual choice, but from treating these as independent decisions instead of a genuinely coherent, mutually reinforcing set.

2Key Ideas

  • The Strategy Choice Cascade. Winning Aspiration → Where to Play → How to Win → Capabilities Needed → Management Systems, each choice flowing logically from and reinforcing the others.
  • Strategy means making real choices, not listing aspirations. A genuine strategy commits to specific markets, customers, and a specific way of winning — not a broad ambition to "compete everywhere."
  • Where to play and how to win must fit together. A chosen market segment only makes sense paired with a specific, coherent way of winning within it — the two can't be decided independently.
  • Capabilities must match the chosen "how to win." A strategy that requires capabilities the organization doesn't have (and hasn't planned to build) isn't really executable, regardless of how sound it looks on paper.
  • Strategy is testable, not just aspirational. A good strategy should generate a small number of clear, falsifiable assumptions that can be tested against real market feedback.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Strategy Choice Cascade

Five interlocking choices: what winning looks like, where to compete, how to win there, what capabilities that requires, and what systems sustain those capabilities — treated as one coherent set, not five independent decisions.

Leadership translation: Choosing to focus on strategic accounts (where to play) only makes sense paired with a specific way of winning there (deep relationship investment, technical expertise) — and that, in turn, requires specific capabilities (dedicated account management, technical training) to actually deliver.

Strategy as Testable Hypothesis

A real strategic choice implies specific, falsifiable assumptions about the market and customer — treating strategy as a hypothesis to test against reality, rather than a fixed pronouncement, keeps it honest.

Example: If a chosen "how to win" assumes customers will pay a premium for a specific capability, that assumption should be explicitly tested, not just assumed true indefinitely.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Walk through all five cascade choices explicitly, not just the ones that feel easiest
  • Check that "where to play" and "how to win" are genuinely coherent with each other
  • Confirm the organization actually has (or is building) the capabilities the strategy requires

How to do it

  • Write one sentence for each of the five cascade elements for your team's current strategy, and check for gaps
  • Test whether the chosen market segment and the chosen way of winning there are actually consistent with each other
  • List the capabilities the strategy requires and honestly assess which ones are missing today

When to apply it

  • Annual and multi-year strategic planning
  • Evaluating whether a new market or segment fits the existing strategy
  • Diagnosing why a strategy isn't translating into results despite sound individual pieces

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Strategy is five interlocking choices, not one big decision.
  • Where you compete and how you win have to fit together.
  • A strategy that needs capabilities you don't have isn't really executable yet.
  • Strategy should generate testable assumptions, not just aspirations.

6Real-World Applications

Reviewing Current Strategy for Gaps

Walking through the five cascade choices explicitly for a team's go-to-market strategy often surfaces a missing or inconsistent link — commonly a mismatch between the chosen "how to win" and the actual capabilities in place.

Evaluating a New Market Segment

Before pursuing a new segment, checking whether the existing "how to win" still applies there — or whether a new one is needed — prevents a strategy from becoming internally incoherent.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Treating the five cascade choices as independent instead of one coherent set.
  • Choosing a market segment and a way of winning that don't actually fit together.
  • Committing to a strategy that requires capabilities you haven't built or planned for.
