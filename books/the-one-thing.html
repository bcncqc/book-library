Focus · Productivity

The One Thing

Gary Keller & Jay Papasan
Extraordinary results come from narrowing focus to the single most important action right now — not from doing more things, but from doing the one thing that makes everything else easier or unnecessary.

1Core Thesis

Keller and Papasan argue that multitasking and long to-do lists are myths of productivity — real extraordinary results come from repeatedly asking a single focusing question and acting on the answer: "What's the ONE thing I can do such that by doing it, everything else becomes easier or unnecessary?" Success is built by going small — narrowing focus down to the single most important priority in any given moment, day, or goal, rather than spreading effort across many "important" things at once.

2Key Ideas

  • The Focusing Question. "What's the ONE thing I can do such that by doing it, everything else becomes easier or unnecessary?" — a repeatable filter for any goal, day, or task list.
  • Multitasking is a myth. The brain doesn't truly multitask on cognitively demanding work — it switches rapidly, losing efficiency and quality each time, despite feeling productive.
  • Success leaves clues, and it's sequential, not simultaneous. Extraordinary results come from doing the right thing, then the next right thing, one at a time — not juggling many priorities at once.
  • The Domino Effect. Like a chain of dominoes, one well-chosen priority action can knock down a whole sequence of subsequent obstacles or goals.
  • Time-block your One Thing. Protecting dedicated, uninterrupted time for the single highest-priority action is more effective than trying to fit it in around everything else.
  • Balance is a myth; counterbalancing is the real skill. Perfectly even attention across every life domain at all times isn't realistic — the goal is to counterbalance over time, giving disproportionate focus where it currently matters most.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Focusing Question

A single repeatable question narrows an overwhelming list of possible priorities down to the one action that would make the most difference right now — applicable at the scale of a career, a year, a month, or a single day.

Leadership translation: Rather than tackling five initiatives at once, asking "what one thing, if done, would make the other four easier or unnecessary?" often reveals a clear sequencing priority.

The Domino Effect

Priorities aren't independent — the right first action often removes or simplifies several subsequent obstacles, the way one falling domino can topple a long chain.

Example: Fixing a core process bottleneck first can make several downstream initiatives dramatically easier or even unnecessary.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Apply the Focusing Question to your goals, week, and day, not just your to-do list
  • Time-block dedicated, protected time for your One Thing before anything else fills the calendar
  • Sequence priorities rather than trying to tackle them simultaneously

How to do it

  • Each morning, name the one task that would make the rest of the day easier or unnecessary, and do it first
  • Block time on the calendar for that one task before other meetings get scheduled around it
  • When facing multiple "important" initiatives, rank them by domino effect rather than urgency alone

When to apply it

  • Daily and weekly planning
  • Setting the top priority for a quarter or major initiative
  • Any time a full plate of "important" work makes it hard to know where to start

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Multitasking feels productive but isn't.
  • Extraordinary results come from doing one thing at a time, well, in sequence.
  • The right first priority can make several others easier or unnecessary.
  • Protect time for your one thing before the calendar fills up around it.

6Real-World Applications

Sequencing Competing Priorities

When a growing team has multiple important initiatives competing for attention, asking which one has the biggest domino effect on the others can clarify a sequencing decision that would otherwise feel arbitrary.

Protecting Deep Work Time

Time-blocking a single highest-priority task before the calendar fills with meetings directly applies the book's core discipline to a busy leadership schedule.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Trying to tackle several "important" priorities simultaneously.
  • Letting the calendar fill up before protecting time for the highest-priority task.
  • Ranking priorities by urgency alone instead of by their domino effect on everything else.
