Behavioral Economics · Incentives

Freakonomics

Steven D. Levitt & Stephen J. Dubner
People respond to incentives — but often not the ones you'd expect, and the honest data frequently contradicts conventional wisdom about why people really do what they do.

1Core Thesis

Levitt and Dubner argue that most conventional explanations for human behavior are convenient stories, not what the data actually shows — and that rigorously following incentives and data, rather than assumptions, routinely uncovers a very different (and often counterintuitive) truth. Across disparate case studies, the throughline is that behavior is shaped by incentives more than morality or intention, and that the real incentive at play is often hidden beneath the stated one.

2Key Ideas

  • Incentives drive behavior, and they come in three flavors. Economic, social, and moral incentives all shape behavior, often in combination, and people respond to whichever is strongest in a given moment.
  • Conventional wisdom is often wrong — check the data. Widely accepted explanations for social phenomena frequently don't survive contact with rigorous data analysis.
  • Correlation isn't causation, and finding the real cause takes discipline. Two things moving together doesn't mean one causes the other — a recurring trap the book returns to across its case studies.
  • People and institutions "cheat" when the incentive to do so outweighs the risk of being caught. Cheating isn't a character flaw exclusive to a few bad actors — it's a predictable response to a given incentive structure.
  • Information asymmetry shapes outcomes. Experts often benefit from knowing more than their clients do, and that asymmetry itself can distort the advice given.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Three Types of Incentives

Economic (money), social (reputation, belonging), and moral (doing the right thing) incentives all compete to shape a given decision — understanding which is dominant in a specific context explains behavior better than assuming one type applies universally.

Leadership translation: A compensation plan that only accounts for economic incentive can be undermined by a stronger social incentive (e.g., peer reputation) pulling in a different direction.

Correlation vs. Causation

Two variables moving together is not evidence that one causes the other — a third factor may drive both, or the causal arrow may run the opposite direction than assumed.

Example: A metric that improves alongside a new initiative isn't proof the initiative caused the improvement without ruling out other explanations.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Identify the real incentive structure behind a behavior before trying to change it
  • Question conventional explanations and check what the actual data shows
  • Watch for correlation being mistaken for causation in your own reporting

How to do it

  • Before redesigning an incentive or comp plan, map which type of incentive (economic, social, moral) is actually driving the current behavior
  • When a "known" explanation for a pattern is offered in a meeting, ask what data actually supports it
  • Before crediting an initiative for an improved metric, ask what else changed at the same time

When to apply it

  • Designing or auditing incentive structures (comp plans, contests, recognition programs)
  • Evaluating whether a new process actually caused a result, or just coincided with it
  • Any time "everyone knows" is offered as the explanation for a trend

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • People respond to incentives, often not the ones you'd assume.
  • "Everyone knows" explanations often don't survive real data.
  • Two things happening together doesn't mean one caused the other.
  • Cheating is a predictable response to incentives, not just a character issue.

6Real-World Applications

Designing Incentive Plans

Before rolling out a new comp or contest structure, mapping which incentive type is actually likely to drive the intended behavior — and which unintended behavior it might drive instead — can prevent a costly misfire.

Evaluating What Actually Worked

When a metric improves after a change, checking for other factors that shifted at the same time avoids over-crediting an initiative that may not have been the real cause.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Designing an incentive plan around only the economic lever while ignoring social or moral incentives at play.
  • Accepting "everyone knows" explanations without checking the underlying data.
  • Crediting an initiative for a result without ruling out other causes.
