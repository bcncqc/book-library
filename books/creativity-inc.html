Creative Leadership · Candor

Creativity, Inc.

Ed Catmull
Protecting creative and organizational honesty requires deliberately building structures — like Pixar's Braintrust — that make candid feedback safe, because left alone, hierarchy and fear of conflict will suppress the truth every time.

1Core Thesis

Catmull, co-founder of Pixar, argues that creative and organizational excellence isn't primarily about hiring brilliant people — it's about building structures that let honest feedback surface and actually get heard, because without deliberate structure, hierarchy, fear, and politeness will suppress the candor that quality work requires. His central practical example is Pixar's "Braintrust" — a group that reviews work-in-progress and gives direct, unfiltered feedback, with the explicit norm that the feedback belongs to the group, not any one person's authority, and the director retains full authority over what to do with it.

2Key Ideas

  • Early work is always bad — that's not a failure signal. Every successful creative project starts rough; the deciding factor is whether the organization has a process to iteratively improve it, not whether the first draft was good.
  • Candor requires deliberate structure, not just good intentions. Left to natural social dynamics, people soften feedback to preserve relationships — genuine candor has to be actively built and protected as a process.
  • The Braintrust: feedback without authority. A structured peer review group gives direct, honest feedback, but explicitly has no authority to mandate changes — the creator retains full ownership and decision rights, which paradoxically makes candor easier to receive.
  • Fear of failure is more damaging than failure itself. An organization that punishes failure harshly suppresses the very risk-taking and honesty that creative and operational excellence require.
  • Protect new, fragile ideas from premature judgment. A new idea in its early, "ugly baby" stage needs protection from harsh evaluation criteria that would be fair to apply to a mature, polished one.
  • Hierarchy naturally distorts honest communication. The more senior someone is, the less honest feedback they tend to receive by default — leaders have to actively work against this dynamic.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Braintrust: Candor Without Authority

A structured peer group gives direct, unfiltered feedback on work in progress, but has no power to mandate the changes it suggests — separating honest input from enforced authority makes the feedback easier to hear and use well.

Leadership translation: A peer review process where colleagues give direct feedback on a proposal, without any one of them having authority to force a change, can surface more honest input than a single manager's top-down review.

The Ugly Baby

New ideas in their earliest form are almost always rough and unimpressive — evaluating them by the same standard as a mature, polished idea kills good ideas before they have a chance to develop.

Example: A rough first draft of a new process shouldn't be judged as harshly as a mature, tested one — it needs room to iterate before being fairly evaluated.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Build a deliberate structure for candid peer feedback, separate from managerial authority
  • Protect early-stage ideas from being judged by mature-idea standards
  • Actively work against the natural tendency for honest feedback to soften as seniority increases

How to do it

  • Set up a peer review process where feedback is direct but the decision authority stays with the person doing the work
  • Explicitly frame early drafts of a new process or initiative as "early stage" to protect them from premature harsh judgment
  • Regularly ask a trusted peer or report what they're hesitant to tell you directly

When to apply it

  • Reviewing new initiatives, SOPs, or strategic proposals in early draft form
  • Building a feedback culture in a newly restructured team
  • Actively counteracting the tendency for senior leaders to receive filtered, softened information

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Every early draft is rough — that's normal, not a failure.
  • Candid feedback doesn't happen naturally — it has to be structurally protected.
  • Separating feedback from authority makes it easier to give and receive honestly.
  • Fear of failure suppresses the honesty and risk-taking that quality work requires.

6Real-World Applications

Reviewing New SOPs or Initiatives

Building a peer-review structure for new process drafts — where feedback is direct but decision authority stays with the owner — can surface better input than a single top-down manager review.

Counteracting Filtered Feedback as a Leader

Regularly and explicitly asking a trusted report what they're hesitant to say directly helps counteract the natural tendency for honest information to get softened as it travels up the hierarchy.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Judging an early-stage idea by mature-idea standards and killing it prematurely.
  • Assuming candor will happen naturally without deliberate structure to protect it.
  • Punishing failure harshly, which suppresses the honesty and risk-taking quality work needs.
