Strategy · Prioritization

Deep Dive

Rich Horwath
Strategy isn't a plan or a stack of goals — it's a disciplined set of choices about where to compete and where not to, made through a repeatable thinking process instead of a once-a-year offsite.

1Core Thesis

Most organizations confuse strategy with planning: they list goals, assign owners, and call it strategy. Horwath argues real strategy is a small number of deliberate choices about where to compete, how to win there, and — just as important — what to stop doing. The "deep dive" is a structured thinking process (not a single meeting) that forces leaders to gather real insight before choosing, filter ideas against a small set of criteria, and commit resources to the few things that will actually move the business, rather than spreading effort evenly across everything that seems reasonable.

2Key Ideas

  • Strategy is a set of choices, not a list of goals. A goal ("grow revenue 20%") isn't a strategy; the choice about which customers, offerings, and channels you'll use to get there is.
  • Insight before choice. Good strategic choices come from systematically gathering perspective on customers, competitors, and your own capabilities — not from opinion in a conference room.
  • Strategy is knowing what NOT to do. Saying yes to everything reasonable is the most common way strategy quietly disappears; a real strategic choice always closes off other options.
  • Straddling fails. Trying to be the low-cost option and the premium, differentiated option at once usually satisfies neither position — pick a lane.
  • Filter ideas against a small number of criteria. Differentiation and profit impact are the two that matter most — an idea that's interesting but doesn't clear both bars shouldn't get resourced.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Strategic Filter

Before committing resources to an initiative, run it through a small set of hard questions: does it differentiate us, and does it improve profitability? Ideas that are merely "good" but fail the filter are the ones that quietly drain a team's capacity.

Leadership translation: A new initiative that sounds good but doesn't clearly differentiate the business or improve margin is exactly the kind of thing that should be cut before it consumes a quarter of a growing team's bandwidth.

Straddling

Attempting to hold two incompatible positions at once (e.g., premium and low-cost) usually erodes both — competitors who commit fully to one position tend to beat the straddler at each.

Example: Trying to serve both high-touch strategic accounts and high-volume transactional ones with the identical sales motion usually serves neither segment well.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Separate "goals" from actual strategic choices in your planning documents
  • Gather real insight (customer, competitor, capability) before choosing priorities
  • Explicitly list what you're choosing NOT to pursue this cycle

How to do it

  • Run every proposed initiative through the differentiation + profit-impact filter before resourcing it
  • Hold a structured "deep dive" session per major decision rather than one annual planning day
  • Write down 2-3 things you're deliberately stopping or de-prioritizing alongside the new priorities

When to apply it

  • Annual and quarterly planning cycles
  • Evaluating a new market segment, product line, or channel
  • Any time the team's plate is full and everything feels "important"

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • A goal is not a strategy — the choice about how you'll get there is.
  • Real strategy always says no to something.
  • Trying to win on two contradictory positions usually loses on both.
  • Good choices come from real insight, not from whoever argues loudest in the room.

6Real-World Applications

Quarterly Planning

Before locking in next quarter's priorities, run each candidate initiative through the differentiation + profit filter — and write down what you're explicitly not doing, not just what you are.

Segmenting Accounts or Territories

If your team is straddling a high-touch strategic motion and a high-volume transactional one with the same playbook, this is the framework for deciding which position to actually commit to.

New Initiative Requests

When a manager proposes a new project, ask the two filter questions before agreeing to resource it — this alone eliminates a large share of well-intentioned but low-impact work.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Calling a list of goals a "strategy" without the underlying choices about how to win.
  • Saying yes to every reasonable-sounding initiative instead of filtering hard.
  • Straddling two incompatible market positions instead of committing to one.
  • Skipping the insight-gathering step and choosing priorities from opinion alone.
