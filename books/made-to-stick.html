Made to Stick

Chip Heath & Dan Heath
Ideas that stick share six traits — they're simple, unexpected, concrete, credible, emotional, and told as a story — and any message can be engineered to have more of them.

1Core Thesis

The Heath brothers set out to answer why some ideas endure and spread (urban legends, proverbs, iconic ad campaigns) while carefully researched, important messages are often forgotten within minutes. Their answer, distilled into the acronym SUCCES, is that sticky ideas consistently share six traits regardless of subject matter — and that these traits can be deliberately engineered into any message, not just discovered accidentally in naturally compelling stories.

2Key Ideas

  • SUCCES: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories. Six traits that show up again and again in ideas that stick, usable as a deliberate checklist for any message.
  • Find the core, then strip everything else. A sticky message usually requires ruthlessly cutting a complex idea down to its single most important core, not adding more detail for completeness.
  • Unexpectedness creates attention, and a "knowledge gap" sustains it. Breaking an expected pattern captures attention; creating a gap between what someone knows and wants to know keeps them engaged until it's closed.
  • Concrete beats abstract. Vivid, sensory, specific language is dramatically more memorable and actionable than abstract principles or jargon.
  • Credibility doesn't require statistics — it can come from vivid detail or testable claims. A specific, checkable detail is often more persuasive than an authoritative-sounding statistic.
  • Emotion drives people to care, and stories give them a simulation to act on. Facts alone rarely move people to act; connecting to a genuine emotion and a concrete story about what to do usually does.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

SUCCES

Simple (find the core), Unexpected (break the expected pattern), Concrete (vivid and specific), Credible (testable or vivid detail), Emotional (make people feel something), Stories (give a simulation to act from) — a checklist for engineering stickiness into any message.

Leadership translation: An internal announcement about a structural change lands better when it leads with one simple core message, a concrete example of what changes day-to-day, and a short story illustrating the impact — not a comprehensive bulleted list of every detail.

The Curse of Knowledge

Once you know something well, it's genuinely difficult to imagine what it's like not to know it — experts consistently overestimate how clear their explanations are to a less-informed audience.

Example: A leader explaining a new process using internal shorthand assumes clarity that a newer team member simply doesn't have yet.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Strip any important message down to its single core idea before adding detail
  • Use concrete, specific language and examples instead of abstract principles
  • Pair the message with a short story or vivid scenario, not just facts

How to do it

  • Before sending a comms piece, write the single sentence that matters most and lead with it
  • Replace an abstract claim ("improve efficiency") with a specific, concrete example of what changes
  • Add one short, real story that illustrates the point rather than relying on data alone

When to apply it

  • Internal announcements about structural or process change
  • Pitches to partners or accounts
  • Training material meant to actually be remembered and applied later

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • A sticky message has a single clear core, not comprehensive detail.
  • Concrete, specific language is more memorable than abstract principles.
  • Facts alone rarely move people — emotion and story do.
  • Once you know something well, you overestimate how clear you're being to others.

6Real-World Applications

Announcing Structural Change

Leading a restructure announcement with one simple core message and a concrete example of what changes day-to-day, rather than a comprehensive list of every detail, applies SUCCES directly.

Training and Onboarding Material

Checking training content for the Curse of Knowledge — jargon or shorthand that feels obvious to the writer but isn't to a new hire — improves how much actually sticks.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Adding comprehensive detail instead of stripping a message down to its core.
  • Relying on abstract language instead of concrete, vivid examples.
  • Assuming your audience shares your context (the Curse of Knowledge).
