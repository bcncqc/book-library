Change Management · Adaptability

Who Moved My Cheese?

Spencer Johnson
Change is inevitable and constant — the people and organizations that thrive are the ones who anticipate it, let go of what no longer works quickly, and move before comfort turns into denial.

1Core Thesis

Told as a short parable about two mice and two "littlepeople" reacting differently to their cheese supply disappearing, Johnson's core argument is that most resistance to change comes from comfort and fear, not from the change itself being genuinely bad. The characters who anticipate change, notice the warning signs early, and act quickly once change happens fare far better than those who cling to "the way things have always been" out of fear or denial.

2Key Ideas

  • Change happens whether you're ready or not. Expecting the current situation to last indefinitely is itself a risk, not a safe default.
  • Anticipate change by watching for early warning signs. The supply of "cheese" (whatever is currently working) rarely disappears without warning — the people who monitor for small signs move earlier and with less pain.
  • Fear of change is usually worse than the change itself. Imagined consequences of adapting are often scarier than the reality once you actually move.
  • Holding onto what's gone delays finding what's next. Time spent denying that circumstances have changed is time not spent adapting to the new reality.
  • Movement, even small, builds momentum. Taking a first small step toward adapting reduces fear and builds the confidence needed for the next step.
  • Enjoy the change, and be ready to change again quickly. Comfort with the new normal shouldn't become the next thing you cling to — the cheese moves again.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Sniff, Scurry, Hem, and Haw

The four characters represent different responses to change: Sniff (anticipates change early), Scurry (acts quickly once change is confirmed), Hem (denies and resists change), Haw (eventually adapts, but slowly and with more fear than necessary).

Leadership translation: A team member who notices early signals that a process is breaking down and adjusts (Sniff/Scurry) fares better than one who insists the old way should keep working (Hem) despite mounting evidence.

Fear of Change vs. the Change Itself

The anticipated pain of adapting is usually worse than the actual experience of doing it — naming that gap explicitly can help move someone from denial to action faster.

Example: A team dreading a new tool or process often finds the actual transition less painful than the anticipation once they finally start.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Watch for early warning signs that the current way of working is breaking down
  • Move quickly once change is confirmed, rather than lingering in denial
  • Take a small first step to build momentum toward a bigger adaptation

How to do it

  • Regularly ask what small signals might indicate the current approach is starting to break down
  • When change is confirmed, identify the smallest first action you can take today rather than waiting for a full plan
  • Name the specific feared consequence of change out loud, then check it against what's actually happened once you move

When to apply it

  • Leading a team through a restructure, process change, or market shift
  • Coaching someone who's stuck resisting an obviously needed change
  • Personal adaptation to a new role, tool, or way of working

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Change is constant — expecting things to stay the same is the actual risk.
  • Watching for early warning signs lets you move before things get painful.
  • Fear of change is usually worse than the change itself.
  • A small first step builds the momentum needed for a bigger adaptation.

6Real-World Applications

Leading a Team Through Restructuring

Naming the specific feared consequence of a change out loud, and encouraging a small first step rather than waiting for full comfort, helps move a team from denial toward adaptation faster.

Coaching Resistance to a New Process

Someone stuck resisting a new SOP or tool often benefits from identifying the smallest possible first action, rather than confronting the change as one large, intimidating shift.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming the current way of working will simply continue indefinitely.
  • Ignoring early warning signs that a change is coming.
  • Staying in denial once change has clearly already happened.
