Innovation · Disruption Theory

The Innovator's Solution

Clayton M. Christensen
Disruptive innovations don't win by beating incumbents at their existing game — they win by starting in an overlooked segment (low-end or new-market) and improving until the mainstream can no longer ignore them.

1Core Thesis

Christensen's follow-up to The Innovator's Dilemma shifts from diagnosing why successful companies get disrupted to prescribing how to build and manage disruptive growth deliberately. His core argument is that disruptive innovations typically start by serving customers overlooked by incumbents — either overserved low-end customers who don't need all the current features, or entirely new customers who weren't consuming the category at all — and improve steadily until they're good enough to take over the mainstream market, by which point incumbents' existing capabilities and incentives make it very difficult for them to respond effectively.

2Key Ideas

  • Disruption starts at the low end or in new markets. Successful disruptors typically don't attack incumbents head-on in their strongest segment — they start where incumbents have no incentive to compete.
  • Sustaining vs. disruptive innovation. Sustaining innovations improve products for existing demanding customers (where incumbents usually win); disruptive innovations create simpler, cheaper, or more accessible alternatives for overlooked segments (where incumbents are structurally disadvantaged to respond).
  • Jobs to be Done. Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to do a specific job in their life; understanding the actual job a customer is hiring a product for reveals opportunities competitors analyzing demographics alone would miss.
  • Incumbents are trapped by their own success. An incumbent's existing cost structure, sales force incentives, and customer base make responding to a low-end disruptor structurally unattractive, even when the threat is visible.
  • Resources, Processes, and Values (RPV) shape what an organization can and can't do. An organization's capabilities aren't just about the resources it has — its processes and values also determine what kinds of innovation it's structurally capable of pursuing.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Jobs to Be Done

Customers "hire" a product or service to accomplish a specific job in their life or work — understanding the actual job, rather than just demographic or product-category data, reveals what really drives adoption and where an overlooked opportunity might exist.

Leadership translation: Understanding the actual job a customer segment is trying to get done — not just their stated feature requests — can reveal an underserved need a competitor's feature list misses entirely.

Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation

Sustaining innovation improves along dimensions existing customers already value (where incumbents have every incentive and capability to win); disruptive innovation creates a new value proposition for an overlooked segment (where incumbents are structurally reluctant to compete).

Example: A simpler, lower-cost alternative aimed at a currently underserved customer segment can gain a foothold that a feature-for-feature competitor to the market leader never could.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Identify the actual "job" customers are hiring your offering to do, not just their stated feature requests
  • Look for overlooked segments (overserved low-end or non-consumers) rather than attacking incumbents head-on
  • Assess whether your organization's processes and incentives structurally support the innovation you're pursuing

How to do it

  • Interview customers about the specific situation and job they're solving for, not just what features they say they want
  • Map which customer segments are currently overserved or entirely unaddressed by existing solutions
  • Check whether your team's current incentives and processes actually support pursuing an overlooked-segment opportunity

When to apply it

  • Evaluating a new market segment or simplified offering
  • Understanding why customers really choose (or don't choose) your solution
  • Assessing whether your organization is structurally capable of pursuing a genuinely disruptive opportunity

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Customers "hire" products to do a specific job — understand the job, not just the feature list.
  • Disruptors usually win by starting where incumbents have no incentive to compete.
  • An incumbent's own success can trap it into ignoring a real threat until it's too late.
  • An organization's processes and values shape what kinds of innovation it can actually pursue.

6Real-World Applications

Understanding Why Customers Really Buy

Interviewing a customer segment about the actual job they're hiring a solution to do — rather than relying on stated feature requests alone — can reveal an underserved need competitors are missing.

Evaluating a New, Simpler Offering

Considering whether a simplified, lower-cost version of an offering could win an overlooked segment — rather than only competing feature-for-feature against established competitors — reflects the disruption pattern directly.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Focusing only on stated feature requests instead of the underlying job customers are trying to get done.
  • Attacking a larger competitor head-on in their strongest segment instead of finding an overlooked one.
  • Assuming your organization can pursue any innovation opportunity without checking whether its processes and incentives actually support it.
