Strategy · Competitive Positioning

The Art of War

Sun Tzu
The supreme skill is winning without fighting — through preparation, positioning, and information advantage secured long before the engagement.

1Core Thesis

War (and by extension any competitive contest) is won or lost before the battle begins, through terrain, timing, information, and positioning. Sun Tzu treats direct confrontation as the costly, inferior option — the highest form of skill is achieving your objective through superior position, deception, and preparation so that the actual clash barely needs to happen or resolves quickly in your favor. Know yourself, know the opponent, and know the ground, and outcomes stop being a matter of chance.

2Key Ideas

  • Win before you fight. The best strategists secure victory through positioning and preparation, making the actual engagement close to a formality — not through superior force in a head-on clash.
  • Know yourself and know your opponent. Accurate self-assessment (your own capacity, resources, morale) is as important as intelligence on the competitor — most losses come from an inflated view of one's own position.
  • Terrain dictates strategy. The ground you fight on (market conditions, timing, home turf vs. away) constrains what's possible — a strategy that ignores terrain is wishful thinking, not planning.
  • Deception and misdirection. "All warfare is based on deception" — showing strength where you're weak and weakness where you're strong shapes the opponent's decisions before they're made.
  • Speed and decisiveness beat prolonged engagement. Long, drawn-out conflicts drain resources and morale on both sides; Sun Tzu repeatedly warns against wars of attrition.
  • Adapt to changing conditions. Rigid plans fail against a live opponent — strategy has to flex in response to real-time information, the way water takes the shape of its container.
  • Leadership sets the tone for the whole force. A commander's discipline, calm, and clarity under pressure directly determines whether the troops hold formation or break.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Five Constant Factors

Sun Tzu's diagnostic for assessing any competitive situation: the Way (shared purpose/morale), Heaven (timing, external conditions), Earth (terrain/positioning), Command (leadership quality), and Method (discipline and process). Whoever scores higher across these factors wins before the fight starts.

Sales-leadership translation: Before entering a new market or account, assess: does the team believe in the plan (Way)? Is the timing right (Heaven)? Do we have positional advantage — relationship, incumbency (Earth)? Is leadership aligned and credible (Command)? Is the process disciplined (Method)? Weak on two or more, and you're fighting uphill.

Win Without Fighting

The highest strategic achievement is winning through position and reputation alone, avoiding a costly direct clash. Applies to any situation where you can shape the outcome through preparation rather than a head-to-head contest.

Example: Rather than fighting a channel partner over pricing terms every cycle, build enough structural leverage (volume commitments, exclusivity, service quality) that the terms are favorable by default.

Know Yourself, Know the Enemy

A 2x2 of self-knowledge and opponent-knowledge: know both and you win consistently; know only yourself and your odds are even; know neither and every engagement is a gamble.

Example: Going into a competitive account review without a clear-eyed read on your own team's actual capacity (not the aspirational version) is as dangerous as not knowing the competitor's position.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Assess the Five Constant Factors before committing to a major initiative
  • Build positional advantage before the confrontation, not during it
  • Get honest intelligence on your own team's real capacity

How to do it

  • Run a pre-mortem on morale, timing, positioning, leadership, and process before launching
  • Secure the structural advantages (data, relationships, exclusivity) ahead of any negotiation
  • Separate what the team can do from what you wish they could do when planning

When to apply it

  • Before entering a competitive account or channel negotiation
  • When designing the sequencing of a major org change
  • Any time the instinct is to "just push harder" instead of repositioning first

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Prepare before you engage. The fight is mostly decided by what happened beforehand.
  • Know your own weaknesses honestly. Overestimating your position is the most common cause of loss.
  • Don't fight long, grinding battles if you can avoid them — attrition costs both sides.
  • Adapt to the actual situation, not the plan you made before you had current information.

6Real-World Applications

Competitive Account Strategy

Before a contested renewal or competitive bid, run the Five Constant Factors assessment on your position vs. the competitor's — decide where you have real advantage and build the engagement around that, rather than matching them point for point.

Org Change Sequencing

Introduce structural change (like a coverage redesign after a departure) when morale, timing, and positioning support it — not just when it becomes administratively necessary. Terrain matters as much as the plan.

Negotiating with Channel Partners

Build leverage (volume data, alternatives, relationship depth) before the negotiation starts. Walking into a pricing conversation without pre-built positional advantage is fighting on the opponent's terrain.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overestimating your own position going into a negotiation or competitive review.
  • Committing to a prolonged fight (price war, drawn-out dispute) when a faster resolution serves you better.
  • Ignoring "terrain" — launching a change at the wrong time, ignoring morale or external conditions.
  • Rigid adherence to the original plan after the situation on the ground has clearly changed.
