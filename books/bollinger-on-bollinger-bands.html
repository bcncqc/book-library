Bollinger on Bollinger Bands

John Bollinger
Price volatility is not constant — it expands and contracts in cycles, and a band that widens and narrows with that volatility tells you more than a fixed price target ever could.

1Core Thesis

Bollinger, creator of the indicator that bears his name, argues that relative price levels (is a price high or low compared to its recent range?) are more useful for trading decisions than absolute price levels alone. Bollinger Bands plot a moving average with two bands above and below it set by standard deviation, so the bands automatically widen during volatile periods and narrow during quiet ones. The core trading insight is that periods of low volatility (a "squeeze") tend to precede periods of high volatility, and price touching or exceeding a band is a signal about relative extremity, not an automatic buy or sell.

2Key Ideas

  • Bands adapt to volatility. Because the bands are set by standard deviation from a moving average, they automatically widen in volatile markets and tighten in calm ones — a fixed-percentage band can't do this.
  • Relative price matters more than absolute price. Whether a price is high or low relative to its recent range is more actionable than whether it's simply "up" or "down."
  • The Squeeze. A period of unusually low volatility (bands pinching tight) often precedes a sharp move — the direction isn't predicted, but the coming volatility often is.
  • Tags aren't automatic signals. A price touching the upper or lower band is a signal to look closer, not an automatic sell/buy — Bollinger pairs band position with other indicators (like volume or momentum) to confirm.
  • No single indicator should be used alone. Bollinger explicitly recommends combining bands with a non-correlated confirming indicator rather than trading off bands in isolation.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Volatility Cycles

Markets alternate between calm and volatile periods; low volatility tends not to persist indefinitely, and a tightening band range is often a precursor to expansion.

General translation: Periods of unusual calm — in markets or in a business — are often a setup for a coming period of change, not a stable new normal.

Confirmation, Not Isolation

A single indicator or signal, viewed alone, is prone to false positives; pairing it with an independent, non-correlated confirming signal reduces that risk.

Example: A band touch on rising volume is treated very differently from the same touch on falling volume.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Evaluate price relative to its recent range, not just its absolute level
  • Watch for volatility squeezes as a signal that a bigger move may be coming
  • Pair any single signal with an independent confirming indicator

How to do it

  • Plot a moving average with standard-deviation bands rather than a flat percentage band
  • Flag periods where the band width compresses sharply as elevated-attention windows
  • Never treat a band touch alone as a trade signal — confirm with volume or momentum first

When to apply it

  • Technical analysis of price charts across any liquid market
  • Volatility-based position sizing or risk management
  • Building or evaluating a systematic trading strategy

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Whether a price is "high" or "low" only means something relative to its recent range.
  • Calm periods often precede volatile ones, not the reverse assumption people make.
  • One indicator touching an extreme isn't a signal by itself — confirm it independently.
  • No single tool should carry a trading decision alone.

6Real-World Applications

Personal or Portfolio Investing

Useful as a framework for gauging whether a position is statistically extended relative to its own recent range before acting, rather than reacting to absolute price moves alone.

Decision-Making Discipline Generally

The "confirm with an independent signal" principle transfers well beyond trading — a single positive data point (one good quarter, one strong review) shouldn't carry a decision alone without a second, independent confirming signal.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Treating a band touch as an automatic buy or sell signal without confirmation.
  • Ignoring volatility cycles and assuming a calm period is the new normal.
  • Relying on one indicator in isolation instead of pairing it with a non-correlated one.
