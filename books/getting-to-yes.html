Negotiation · Conflict Resolution

Getting to Yes

Roger Fisher & William Ury
The best negotiations aren't won by whoever holds their position hardest — they come from separating people from the problem and negotiating on underlying interests, not stated positions.

1Core Thesis

Fisher and Ury, developing the Harvard Negotiation Project's core method, argue against both "soft" negotiation (conceding to preserve the relationship) and "hard" positional bargaining (digging in to win). Their alternative, "principled negotiation," separates the people from the problem, focuses on underlying interests rather than stated positions, generates multiple options before deciding, and insists on using objective, independent criteria to resolve disagreements — producing better, more durable outcomes than either soft or hard approaches typically do.

2Key Ideas

  • Separate the people from the problem. Treating the other party as an adversary to be defeated makes the actual problem harder to solve — attack the problem, not the person.
  • Focus on interests, not positions. A stated position ("I want X") often hides a deeper interest ("I need certainty by Friday") — negotiating the interest opens far more possible solutions than defending the position.
  • Generate options for mutual gain before deciding. Brainstorming multiple possible solutions before committing to one avoids prematurely narrowing to a single, often suboptimal, compromise.
  • Insist on objective criteria. When positions conflict, resolving the gap using independent standards (market rate, precedent, expert judgment) is more durable than resolving it through sheer willpower or pressure.
  • Know your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement). Understanding your real walk-away alternative — and the other side's — clarifies what's actually a good deal versus a bad one.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Positions vs. Interests

A position is what someone says they want; an interest is the underlying need driving that position. Negotiating interests opens far more creative solutions than defending fixed positions against each other.

Leadership translation: A partner's stated position of "we need a lower price" might really be an interest in predictable quarterly costs — a volume-based or staged pricing structure can satisfy that interest without simply cutting the number.

BATNA

Your Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement is your actual fallback if this negotiation fails — knowing it clearly (and improving it where possible) is more useful than any single tactic in the room.

Example: Entering a renewal negotiation with a clear-eyed view of your actual alternative if it falls through changes what terms you're willing to accept.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Identify the underlying interest behind a stated position before responding to the position itself
  • Generate multiple possible solutions before locking into one
  • Know your own BATNA clearly before entering a high-stakes negotiation

How to do it

  • Ask "why do you need that specifically?" to surface the interest behind a stated position
  • Brainstorm at least three possible structures before committing to a single proposal
  • Write down your actual walk-away alternative before the negotiation starts, not during it

When to apply it

  • Contract or pricing negotiations with a partner or vendor
  • Internal disagreements over resourcing or priorities
  • Any negotiation where a position feels stuck or adversarial

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • What someone asks for isn't always what they actually need.
  • Attacking the person makes the actual problem harder to solve.
  • More options generated before deciding usually beats an early single compromise.
  • Knowing your real fallback clarifies what's actually a good deal.

6Real-World Applications

Channel Partner or Account Negotiations

Surfacing a partner's underlying interest behind a hard-line position often opens a structure (staged pricing, extended terms, bundled service) that satisfies the real need without simply conceding the number.

Internal Resourcing Disagreements

A stuck internal disagreement over headcount or budget often resolves faster once both sides articulate the underlying interest, not just the stated ask.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Treating the other party as an adversary instead of separating the people from the problem.
  • Responding to a stated position without probing for the underlying interest.
  • Locking into the first workable option instead of generating multiple before deciding.
  • Entering a negotiation without a clear sense of your own BATNA.
