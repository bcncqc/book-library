Competitive Strategy

Michael E. Porter
An industry's underlying structure — not any single company's cleverness — largely determines how much profit is available to be won, and three generic strategies describe the durable ways to capture it.

1Core Thesis

Porter's foundational work argues that competitive strategy has to start with rigorous analysis of an industry's structure — the Five Forces (rivalry, supplier power, buyer power, substitutes, new entrants) — because that structure largely determines the ceiling on profitability regardless of any single company's execution. Within a given structure, Porter identifies three generic strategies for building a durable position: cost leadership, differentiation, and focus — and warns that companies who fail to commit clearly to one ("stuck in the middle") tend to underperform competitors who commit fully to any of the three.

2Key Ideas

  • Five Forces determine industry attractiveness. Competitive rivalry, supplier power, buyer power, threat of substitutes, and threat of new entrants together shape how much profit potential an industry structurally offers.
  • Three generic strategies: cost leadership, differentiation, focus. Cost leadership wins by being the lowest-cost producer; differentiation wins by offering something genuinely unique customers will pay more for; focus wins by serving a narrow segment better than broad competitors can.
  • "Stuck in the middle" underperforms. Companies that don't commit clearly to one generic strategy — trying to be a bit of everything — tend to underperform those that commit fully to any single one.
  • Entry barriers protect profitability. High barriers to new entrants (capital requirements, brand loyalty, switching costs) help preserve an industry's profit potential over time.
  • Buyer and supplier power set the terms of negotiation. Concentrated buyers or suppliers can capture more of an industry's value, independent of how well any single company executes.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Five Forces

Rivalry among existing competitors, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitute products, and threat of new entrants — together these forces determine an industry's structural profit potential, separate from any individual company's skill.

Leadership translation: High buyer concentration in a given account segment explains persistent pricing pressure better than assuming it's purely a sales execution issue.

Three Generic Strategies

Cost Leadership (lowest-cost producer), Differentiation (unique value customers pay more for), and Focus (serving a narrow segment better than broad competitors) — a durable position requires committing clearly to one, not blending them halfway.

Example: A team trying to be both the cheapest option and the most premium, highly differentiated one simultaneously is at risk of being "stuck in the middle," losing to competitors committed fully to either position.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Analyze your industry or market segment's Five Forces before assuming performance is purely about execution
  • Commit clearly to one generic strategy rather than blending cost, differentiation, and focus halfway
  • Evaluate how buyer or supplier concentration affects your actual negotiating position

How to do it

  • Map your market's Five Forces explicitly at least once a year to separate structural headwinds from execution issues
  • Identify which generic strategy your team is actually committed to, and check for signs of being "stuck in the middle"
  • Assess buyer/supplier concentration in your key accounts to understand real negotiating leverage

When to apply it

  • Annual strategic planning and market analysis
  • Diagnosing persistent pricing pressure or margin erosion
  • Clarifying whether the team's positioning is genuinely committed or "stuck in the middle"

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Industry structure sets a ceiling on profitability, independent of execution.
  • There are three durable ways to win: cheapest, most differentiated, or most focused on a niche.
  • Trying to do all three halfway usually underperforms committing fully to one.
  • Buyer and supplier concentration shape your negotiating position structurally.

6Real-World Applications

Diagnosing Margin Pressure

Mapping Five Forces for a specific market segment can reveal that persistent pricing pressure is structural (buyer concentration, low switching costs) rather than purely a sales execution problem.

Clarifying Competitive Positioning

Checking whether a team is genuinely committed to differentiation, cost leadership, or focus — or quietly stuck in the middle — can explain underperformance that looks otherwise unexplained.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming performance issues are purely about execution without checking industry structure.
  • Trying to blend cost leadership and differentiation halfway instead of committing to one.
  • Ignoring buyer or supplier concentration when negotiating terms.
