Good Strategy, Bad Strategy

Richard Rumelt
Most "strategy" is actually a list of goals and inspirational language dressed up as strategy — real strategy requires an honest diagnosis of the actual challenge, a guiding policy, and a set of coherent actions.

1Core Thesis

Rumelt argues that most organizations produce "bad strategy": a fluffy vision statement, a wish-list of goals, and buzzwords, without ever confronting the actual obstacle standing in the way. Good strategy, by contrast, follows a specific structure — the "kernel" — consisting of an honest diagnosis of the challenge, a guiding policy for dealing with it, and a set of coherent, coordinated actions that follow logically from that policy. Bad strategy isn't just weak strategy — it actively avoids the hard work of diagnosis and choice.

2Key Ideas

  • The Kernel: Diagnosis, Guiding Policy, Coherent Action. Good strategy names the actual problem honestly, chooses an overall approach for tackling it, and lines up specific actions that reinforce each other toward that approach.
  • Bad strategy avoids diagnosis. It substitutes a goal or aspiration ("be the market leader") for an honest assessment of the specific obstacle blocking that outcome.
  • Bad strategy is a list of goals dressed up as a plan. A collection of desirable outcomes ("grow revenue," "improve culture") isn't a strategy until it's paired with a specific diagnosis and coherent action.
  • Fluff is not strategy. Restating a common idea using complicated, inspirational-sounding language creates the illusion of insight without providing any real direction.
  • A strategy's actions must be coherent, not just additive. A pile of unrelated initiatives, however individually reasonable, isn't a strategy if they don't reinforce a common approach.
  • Leverage comes from concentrating resources on a pivotal point. Good strategy identifies the specific point where a concentrated push will have disproportionate effect, rather than spreading effort evenly.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

The Kernel

Diagnosis (what's the actual challenge, honestly stated?) → Guiding Policy (the overall approach for dealing with it) → Coherent Action (specific, coordinated steps that follow logically from the policy).

Leadership translation: "Grow NA revenue 20%" is a goal, not a strategy — the kernel requires naming the actual obstacle (e.g., inconsistent process across a newly restructured team), a guiding policy for addressing it (standardize before scaling), and coherent actions that follow (SOPs, metrics, role clarity, in that sequence).

Bad Strategy Red Flags

Fluff (buzzwords substituting for real thinking), failure to face the challenge honestly, mistaking goals for strategy, and a grab-bag of unrelated initiatives that don't reinforce each other.

Example: A strategy deck full of aspirational language but no honest naming of the actual obstacle is a warning sign, regardless of how polished it looks.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Name the actual, specific obstacle honestly before setting goals
  • Choose one guiding policy for addressing that obstacle
  • Check that proposed actions actually reinforce each other, not just sit side by side

How to do it

  • Before writing a strategy doc, write one honest paragraph naming the real obstacle, no euphemisms
  • State the guiding policy in one sentence, and test whether every proposed initiative actually serves it
  • Cut any listed initiative that doesn't clearly reinforce the guiding policy

When to apply it

  • Annual or quarterly strategic planning
  • Reviewing an existing strategy document for whether it's actually a strategy or a goals list
  • Deciding where to concentrate limited resources for maximum leverage

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • A list of goals is not a strategy.
  • Real strategy starts with an honest diagnosis of the actual obstacle.
  • Buzzwords and inspirational language aren't a substitute for real thinking.
  • Scattered initiatives that don't reinforce each other aren't a coherent strategy.

6Real-World Applications

Writing (or Reviewing) a Strategic Plan

Testing a draft strategy doc against the Kernel — is there an honest diagnosis, a guiding policy, and coherent action, or just a list of goals — is a fast, direct way to check whether it's actually a strategy.

Sequencing Priorities

Choosing to standardize process before scaling volume is itself a guiding-policy choice — worth stating explicitly rather than leaving implicit, so every subsequent initiative can be checked against it.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Mistaking a list of goals for an actual strategy.
  • Avoiding an honest diagnosis of the real obstacle in favor of aspirational language.
  • Approving a pile of disconnected initiatives without checking they reinforce one guiding policy.
