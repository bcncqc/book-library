Leadership · Vulnerability & Trust

Dare to Lead

Brené Brown
Vulnerability isn't weakness — it's the birthplace of courage, and daring leadership means showing up and being seen even when the outcome isn't guaranteed.

1Core Thesis

Brown argues that "armored" leadership — projecting certainty, avoiding hard conversations, protecting your image — is a common but ultimately weaker strategy than "daring" leadership, which requires the vulnerability of admitting uncertainty, having hard conversations, and being willing to be wrong in front of people. Trust is built through small, consistent, specific behaviors (not a single grand gesture), and vulnerability is the prerequisite for both innovation and genuine team trust, because you can't have honest feedback, real creativity, or accountability without people first feeling safe enough to be seen.

2Key Ideas

  • Vulnerability is not weakness — it's courage. Showing up without guaranteed outcomes (giving hard feedback, admitting a mistake, asking for help) is the actual definition of brave, not the absence of it.
  • Armored leadership vs. Daring leadership. Armored leaders protect themselves with perfectionism, control, and cynicism. Daring leaders lead with curiosity, rumbling with vulnerability, and staying open to being wrong.
  • The BRAVING framework for trust: Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault (confidentiality), Integrity, Non-judgment, Generosity — trust is built and eroded across these specific, nameable behaviors.
  • Clear is kind, unclear is unkind. Withholding direct feedback to spare short-term discomfort actually does the other person a disservice — vague or absent feedback is the unkind option, not the kind one.
  • Rumbling with vulnerability. A "rumble" is a conversation grounded in curiosity and honesty rather than a scripted, defensive exchange — staying in the rumble means tolerating discomfort instead of retreating to armor.
  • Shame vs. guilt. Shame ("I am bad") shuts down learning and honesty; guilt ("I did something bad") allows for repair and growth. Leaders who create shame-based cultures get less honesty, not more compliance.
  • Trust is built in small moments, not grand gestures. The "marble jar" metaphor — trust accumulates one small consistent behavior at a time and drains quickly with a single significant breach.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Armored vs. Daring Leadership

Armored behaviors — perfectionism, knowing it all, being right, staying in intellectual safety instead of emotional honesty. Daring behaviors — asking for feedback, admitting uncertainty, staying curious under pressure. Every leader has both available in any given moment; the choice is deliberate.

Sales-leadership translation: Presenting the NA restructure to leadership as though every detail is fully resolved (armored) versus openly naming the still-unresolved questions like a specific team's reporting structure (daring) — the second builds more credibility over time, even though it feels riskier in the moment.

BRAVING Trust Inventory

Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault, Integrity, Non-judgment, Generosity — a checklist for diagnosing exactly where trust is breaking down in a relationship or team, rather than treating "trust" as one vague, unfixable thing.

Example: If a direct report doesn't trust you, it might specifically be a Reliability issue (you don't follow through) or an Accountability issue (you don't own your mistakes) — naming the specific dimension makes it fixable.

Clear Is Kind

Withholding direct, specific feedback to avoid discomfort is reframed as the actually unkind choice — it deprives the other person of the information they need to improve or correct course.

Example: Not telling a channel manager clearly that their approach isn't working, out of a desire to be supportive, is unkind by this framework — it delays a correction they need.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Name uncertainty explicitly instead of projecting false confidence
  • Diagnose trust issues against the specific BRAVING dimension, not just "trust is low"
  • Choose clear, direct feedback over comfortable vagueness

How to do it

  • In leadership updates, separate "what's confirmed" from "what's still being worked out" rather than smoothing over the gaps
  • When trust feels off with someone, run the BRAVING checklist to find the actual specific issue
  • Before softening feedback, ask "is this actually kind, or just comfortable for me?"

When to apply it

  • Presenting unresolved org questions (team structure, an undefined role's scope) to leadership
  • Rebuilding trust after a disruptive event like a key departure
  • Any recurring feedback conversation that's been vague instead of direct

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Admitting you don't have it all figured out is braver — and more credible — than pretending you do.
  • Trust breaks down in specific, nameable ways — treat it as diagnosable, not just a vague feeling.
  • Withholding direct feedback to be nice is actually the unkind choice.
  • Trust is built in small, consistent moments, not restored by one big gesture after it's broken.

6Real-World Applications

Presenting Open Org Questions

Rather than presenting the NA restructure as fully resolved, naming the genuinely open questions (team structure, an undocumented role's scope) directly to leadership is daring leadership — rather than presenting them as settled when they're not.

Rebuilding Trust After a Departure

Trust with the remaining team after a disruptive departure gets rebuilt through the BRAVING dimensions specifically — reliability in the coverage plan, clarity on boundaries of the new roles, and non-judgment toward people absorbing new scope.

Direct Feedback Culture

"Clear is kind" reinforces the Radical Candor discipline already in this library — useful shared language when coaching managers who default to vague, comfortable feedback.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Projecting false certainty on unresolved questions to appear more in control than you are.
  • Treating "trust is low" as unfixable instead of diagnosing the specific BRAVING dimension that's broken.
  • Softening feedback to spare short-term discomfort, which delays the correction the person actually needs.
  • Expecting one big gesture to repair trust instead of rebuilding it through small consistent behaviors.
