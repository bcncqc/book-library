Strategy · Enduring Companies

Built to Last

Jim Collins & Jerry Porras
Visionary companies that endure for generations aren't built around a single great idea or leader — they're built around a core ideology preserved fiercely, paired with relentless willingness to change everything else.

1Core Thesis

Collins and Porras studied companies that had thrived for many decades, comparing each to a strong but less enduring competitor, and found that longevity wasn't explained by a single great product idea, a charismatic founder, or being first to market. Instead, enduring companies preserve a core ideology (core values and purpose) with near-religious consistency, while being relentlessly willing to change strategies, products, and practices in service of that unchanging core — a combination the authors call "preserve the core, stimulate progress."

2Key Ideas

  • Preserve the core, stimulate progress. Enduring companies hold their core values and purpose nearly fixed, while aggressively changing everything else (products, strategies, practices) to keep progressing.
  • Core ideology is not just words on a wall. A durable core ideology has to genuinely shape decisions and get reinforced through consistent practice, not just a mission statement.
  • Big Hairy Audacious Goals (BHAGs). A clear, bold, long-term goal — sometimes ambitious to the point of seeming reckless — can galvanize an organization more effectively than a modest, safe target.
  • Cult-like cultures around the core ideology. The most enduring companies have a strong internal culture that reinforces core values intensely, even while remaining highly adaptable in strategy and tactics.
  • Home-grown management, not a single visionary. Enduring companies tend to develop leadership from within over generations rather than depending on one irreplaceable founder or outside star.
  • Good enough never is. A relentless, self-improving discipline — never settling for "we've made it" — sustains long-term performance more than any single strategic decision.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Preserve the Core / Stimulate Progress

Core values and purpose stay nearly fixed across decades, while nearly everything else (strategy, products, structure) is aggressively open to change — durability comes from this specific combination, not from resisting change generally.

Leadership translation: Holding firm on a genuine core value (like customer-first service) while being willing to completely rework the process, team structure, or tools used to deliver it reflects this same pattern at a smaller scale.

BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal)

A bold, clear, long-term goal that's almost uncomfortably ambitious can rally an organization's energy more effectively than an incremental, safe target that doesn't demand real change.

Example: A specific, bold long-term target for a growing commercial team (not just "grow revenue") can concentrate effort more effectively than a vague aspiration.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Define your team's genuine core values, distinct from any specific current strategy or practice
  • Be willing to change strategy, process, and tools aggressively in service of that unchanging core
  • Set one bold, clear long-term goal that demands real change, not incremental improvement

How to do it

  • Write down which values are genuinely non-negotiable versus which practices are just "how we've always done it"
  • When a practice conflicts with a core value, change the practice, not the value
  • Set and communicate one bold, specific long-term goal for the team, not just a modest annual target

When to apply it

  • Defining or reinforcing team culture during a growth or restructuring phase
  • Long-term strategic planning
  • Deciding what to protect versus what to change as the team scales

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Enduring organizations hold their values fixed while changing everything else.
  • A mission statement only matters if it actually shapes real decisions.
  • A bold, specific long-term goal galvanizes more than a safe, modest one.
  • Never settling for "good enough" is itself a durable competitive advantage.

6Real-World Applications

Reinforcing Culture During Restructuring

Distinguishing genuine core values from "how we've always done it" clarifies what should survive a restructure unchanged versus what's actually fine to redesign completely.

Setting a Long-Term Team Goal

Defining one bold, specific long-term goal for the commercial team — rather than a vague aspiration — can concentrate effort and energy more effectively during a growth phase.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Treating every existing practice as sacred instead of distinguishing core values from changeable tactics.
  • Writing a mission statement that doesn't actually shape real decisions.
  • Setting only modest, safe goals that don't demand genuine change.
