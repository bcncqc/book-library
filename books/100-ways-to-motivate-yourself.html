100 Ways to Motivate Yourself

Steve Chandler
Motivation isn't something that happens to you — it's a skill you build through self-talk, ownership of your choices, and small immediate actions, not something you wait to feel.

1Core Thesis

Chandler argues that most people wait to "feel motivated" before acting, when the relationship actually runs the other way — action, especially small immediate action, creates motivation, not the reverse. The book is a collection of short, punchy reframes aimed at replacing victim-language ("I have to," "I can't") with ownership language ("I choose to," "I won't right now"), on the premise that the way you talk to yourself directly shapes what you're capable of doing next.

2Key Ideas

  • Action creates motivation, not the other way around. Waiting to feel ready before starting usually means waiting indefinitely — starting small is what generates the feeling of momentum.
  • Language shapes capability. Replacing "I have to" with "I choose to" isn't just semantics — it restores a sense of agency that changes how a task actually feels to do.
  • Self-talk is a skill, not a fixed trait. The internal narration people run is learned and habitual, which means it can be deliberately retrained.
  • Commitment beats interest. Being "interested" in a goal leaves an exit ramp at the first obstacle; being committed removes it — most stalled motivation is really an interest/commitment gap.
  • Small immediate wins compound. A tiny completed action today builds more real motivation than a large, still-theoretical plan for next month.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Action → Motivation, Not Motivation → Action

Reverses the common assumption: instead of waiting to feel motivated to start, start with a small, low-stakes action first — the feeling of motivation tends to follow, not precede, movement.

Leadership translation: A team stalled on a big initiative usually needs one small, immediately completable step, not more inspiration or another kickoff meeting.

Interested vs. Committed

People who are merely interested in a goal quit at the first real obstacle; people who are committed have already removed that exit option from consideration.

Example: "I'll try to hit the number" is interest; "I'm finding a way to hit the number" is commitment — the language reveals which one is actually in play.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Start with a small action instead of waiting to feel motivated
  • Replace "have to" language with "choose to" language
  • Distinguish genuine commitment from mere interest before assuming a stall is a motivation problem

How to do it

  • Pick the smallest possible next action on a stalled task and do only that today
  • Catch yourself saying "I have to" and consciously restate it as "I'm choosing to, because..."
  • Ask directly: am I committed to this, or just interested? — then act accordingly

When to apply it

  • Any task you keep postponing while "waiting to feel ready"
  • Coaching someone who sounds stuck in victim language about their workload
  • Diagnosing whether a stalled goal is a motivation problem or a commitment problem

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • Don't wait to feel motivated — act first, in something small, and the feeling follows.
  • The words you use with yourself change what feels possible.
  • Most "motivation problems" are really commitment problems.
  • A small completed action beats a big unfinished plan.

6Real-World Applications

Coaching a Stalled Team Member

When someone describes their workload in "have to" language, helping them reframe it as a series of chosen actions — starting with one small completed step — often restores momentum faster than a pep talk.

Your Own Stalled Priorities

A large strategic initiative that's stalled is often better served by identifying the smallest possible next action than by waiting for a burst of inspiration to tackle it fully.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Waiting to "feel like it" before starting a task that's been postponed.
  • Using "have to" language that quietly reinforces a lack of agency.
  • Treating a commitment gap as if it were a motivation gap and trying to fix it with encouragement alone.
