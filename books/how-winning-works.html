Leadership · Team Performance

How Winning Works

Robyn Benincasa
Elite adventure-racing teams win not by having the strongest individual athletes, but by building trust, role clarity, and shared purpose strong enough to survive exhaustion, crisis, and disagreement.

1Core Thesis

Benincasa, a world-champion adventure racer, draws leadership lessons from multi-day expedition races where teams must navigate, problem-solve, and physically endure together under extreme stress. Her central argument: the team that wins isn't the one with the fastest individuals — it's the one whose trust, communication, and shared purpose hold up when things go wrong, because in a long enough race (or business cycle), something always does.

2Key Ideas

  • Talent isn't the differentiator — cohesion is. Teams of strong individuals lose to less-talented teams with better trust and communication once conditions get hard enough.
  • Shared purpose has to survive exhaustion. A team's "why" needs to be strong enough to hold up at hour 40 of a race, not just at the starting line.
  • Trust is built before the crisis, not during it. Waiting until a crisis to establish trust in teammates is too late — it has to already exist when things go wrong.
  • Everyone needs a clear role, and the flexibility to step outside it when needed. Rigid roles fail under real pressure; role clarity plus adaptability is the actual winning combination.
  • Recovery speed matters more than avoiding mistakes. Every long race includes wrong turns and setbacks — the deciding factor is how fast the team recovers, not whether mistakes happen at all.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Cohesion Beats Talent

Past a certain threshold of individual competence, a team's ability to trust, communicate, and recover together outweighs any gap in raw individual talent.

Leadership translation: A commercial team of solid-but-not-superstar performers with high trust and clear communication will often outperform a team of stars who don't coordinate well under pressure.

Recovery Speed

The team that wins isn't the one that avoids every setback — it's the one that recognizes a wrong turn fastest and adjusts without spiraling into blame.

Example: A missed forecast handled with a fast, clear-eyed correction beats a "perfect" quarter followed by a slow, blame-heavy reaction to the next miss.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Invest in trust and communication before you need them in a crisis
  • Give people clear roles, but build in the expectation that they'll flex when needed
  • Measure recovery speed after a setback, not just whether setbacks happened

How to do it

  • Run team debriefs after both wins and losses to build the habit of honest reflection under low stakes
  • Explicitly define roles, and just as explicitly define when someone should step outside theirs to help
  • After a setback, focus the retro on "how fast did we notice and adjust" rather than only "whose fault was it"

When to apply it

  • Building a new or newly restructured team
  • Post-mortems after a missed target or a difficult stretch
  • Any high-pressure period where individual heroics alone won't be enough

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • The most talented team doesn't always win — the most cohesive one does.
  • Trust has to be built before the crisis hits, not during it.
  • Clear roles matter, but so does flexing outside them under pressure.
  • How fast you recover from a mistake matters more than avoiding mistakes entirely.

6Real-World Applications

Building a Newly Restructured Team

After a team absorbs new roles or a reorganization, deliberately building trust and communication norms early — rather than waiting for the first real crisis — pays off later.

Post-Miss Debriefs

Framing a retro around "how fast did we notice and correct" rather than only assigning blame builds the recovery-speed muscle this book emphasizes.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming individual talent alone wins without investing in team cohesion.
  • Waiting until a crisis to build trust that should already exist.
  • Making roles so rigid that no one flexes to help when it's actually needed.
  • Focusing post-mortems only on blame instead of recovery speed.
