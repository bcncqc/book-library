Rework

Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson
Most conventional business wisdom — long hours, detailed plans, big meetings, outside funding — is optional or actively harmful; a smaller, simpler, faster way of working usually beats it.

1Core Thesis

Fried and Hansson, founders of Basecamp (formerly 37signals), argue against nearly every piece of conventional startup and business orthodoxy — that you need a detailed business plan, outside investors, long hours, a large team, or extensive market research before starting. Their alternative philosophy favors starting small, shipping quickly, staying lean, and treating constraints (limited time, limited money, limited people) as creative advantages rather than problems to solve by adding more resources.

2Key Ideas

  • Planning is guessing. Long-range business plans are usually just elaborate guesses — better to build something small now and adjust based on real feedback than to plan extensively before starting.
  • Workaholism is not the same as productivity. Long hours are often a sign of poor prioritization or unclear scope, not dedication — sustainable, focused work beats burnout-driven hustle.
  • Meetings are toxic in excess. Meetings interrupt real work, expand to fill available time, and often could be replaced by a short written update.
  • Say no by default. Every added feature, meeting, or process has a cost — the default answer to a new request should be no unless it clearly earns its place.
  • Build half a product, not a half-assed product. Cutting scope to focus on doing fewer things exceptionally well beats trying to do everything at a mediocre level.
  • Constraints are an advantage, not a limitation. Limited time, money, or people force creative prioritization that abundant resources often let you avoid.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Half a Product, Not a Half-Assed Product

Deliberately cutting scope to focus resources on doing a smaller set of things exceptionally well beats spreading the same resources across a larger scope done mediocrely.

Leadership translation: Rolling out a new process to a smaller pilot group first, done well, tends to produce a better outcome than rolling it out company-wide immediately at lower quality.

Say No by Default

Every meeting, feature, or process added has an ongoing cost that compounds — treating "no" as the default answer, overridden only by a clear case, keeps an organization lean as it grows.

Example: A proposed new recurring meeting should have to justify its ongoing cost, not just its one-time benefit, before being added to the calendar permanently.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Treat long-range plans as rough guesses, not fixed commitments
  • Default to "no" on new meetings, features, or processes unless they clearly earn their place
  • Cut scope deliberately rather than trying to do everything at once

How to do it

  • Build a shorter-term plan and expect to revise it based on real feedback rather than planning further out than useful
  • Before adding a new recurring meeting, require it to justify its ongoing cost, not just a one-time need
  • When rolling out a new initiative, deliberately narrow the scope to what can be done exceptionally well first

When to apply it

  • Planning cycles where long, detailed plans tend to go stale quickly
  • Evaluating whether to add a new recurring meeting or process
  • Rolling out a new initiative across a growing team

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • A long-range plan is usually just an elaborate guess.
  • Long hours aren't the same thing as real productivity.
  • Every meeting or process added has an ongoing cost — default to no.
  • Doing fewer things exceptionally well beats doing everything adequately.

6Real-World Applications

Evaluating New Meetings or Processes

Requiring a new recurring meeting or reporting requirement to justify its ongoing cost before adding it to the calendar keeps a growing team lean rather than accumulating overhead by default.

Piloting Before Full Rollout

Rolling out a new SOP or process to a smaller pilot group first, done well, rather than company-wide immediately, applies the "half a product, not half-assed" principle.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Treating a long-range plan as a fixed commitment rather than a rough guess to revise.
  • Adding meetings or processes by default instead of requiring them to earn their place.
  • Trying to do everything at once instead of narrowing scope to do less, exceptionally well.
