Performance Metrics · Goal-Setting

Measure What Matters

John Doerr
OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) work because they force a separation between the ambitious "what" and the measurable "how we'll know" — and because they're transparent, so everyone can see how their work ladders up.

1Core Thesis

Doerr popularizes the OKR framework (originated at Intel, later adopted widely including at Google) as a goal-setting system built for focus and alignment at speed. An Objective is a qualitative, ambitious statement of what you want to achieve; Key Results are the specific, measurable indicators that prove you got there. Doerr's argument is that most goal-setting fails not from lack of ambition but from vagueness about what "done" actually looks like, and from goals that are set in isolation rather than made visible across the organization.

2Key Ideas

  • Objectives are the "what," Key Results are the "how we'll know." An Objective without measurable Key Results is just an aspiration; Key Results without a clear Objective is just busywork.
  • OKRs should be ambitious, not just achievable. A key result hit 100% of the time every cycle is probably set too low — some stretch and honest miss is expected and healthy.
  • Transparency matters as much as the goals themselves. When OKRs are visible across a team or company, people can see how their work ladders up to bigger objectives, which drives alignment without micromanagement.
  • Fewer, better goals beat a long list. A handful of focused Objectives with 2-4 Key Results each outperforms a long list of scattered priorities.
  • OKRs are separate from compensation. Tying OKRs directly to pay incentivizes sandbagging (setting easy goals) rather than ambition — Doerr argues they should be evaluated, not used to determine bonuses directly.
  • Continuous check-ins beat annual goal-setting. OKRs work best with regular (often quarterly, sometimes more frequent) review and adjustment, not a once-a-year exercise.

3Mental Models & Frameworks

Objective + Key Results

A single ambitious, qualitative Objective paired with 2-4 specific, measurable Key Results that define what success looks like — the structure forces clarity about both direction and proof.

Leadership translation: "Build a repeatable, standardized commercial process" is an Objective; "X SOPs published and adopted," "Y% reduction in escalations," and "Z% of new hires ramped within N days" are the kind of Key Results that would prove it happened.

Stretch Goals and Healthy Misses

A Key Result hit exactly 100% every cycle likely wasn't ambitious enough — 60-70% attainment on a genuinely stretch goal often represents more real progress than 100% on an easy one.

Example: A team consistently "passing" every goal might actually be under-reaching, not over-performing.

4Actionable Playbook

What to do

  • Separate ambitious Objectives from specific, measurable Key Results
  • Make goals visible across the team, not just held by individual managers
  • Review and adjust on a regular cadence, not just once a year

How to do it

  • Write 3-5 Objectives per function, each with 2-4 measurable Key Results
  • Publish OKRs somewhere the whole team can see how their work ladders up
  • Set a quarterly (or more frequent) check-in cadence to score and adjust

When to apply it

  • Building a performance metrics and assessment framework for a growing team
  • Quarterly and annual planning cycles
  • Aligning a newly restructured team around shared, visible priorities

5Key Lessons Broken Down Simply

  • A goal without a measurable proof of success is just a wish.
  • Hitting every goal every time probably means the goals were too easy.
  • Visible goals create alignment without requiring constant check-ins.
  • Fewer, sharper goals beat a long list of scattered ones.

6Real-World Applications

Building the Performance Metrics Framework

OKRs are a directly applicable structure for defining what "good" looks like per role — an ambitious Objective per function, paired with a small number of measurable Key Results, gives the assessment framework real teeth.

Aligning a Newly Restructured Team

Publishing visible, shared OKRs across newly defined roles helps everyone see how their individual work ladders up to the team's bigger objectives, reinforcing structure as the new normal.

7Mistakes to Avoid

  • Setting goals with no measurable Key Results attached.
  • Setting goals so safe that they're hit 100% every cycle.
  • Tying OKRs directly to compensation, which incentivizes sandbagging.
  • Setting goals once a year and never revisiting them.
